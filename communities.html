Priority Communities

Who This
Is Built For

Six communities were identified as priority focus areas based on structural gaps in civic access, representation, and educational investment. The initiative is designed around their specific conditions — not as a generic statewide rollout.

The Six Communities

Priority Focus Areas

These communities were selected because existing civic education systems have consistently underserved them — not because of any deficit in the communities themselves.

Multilingual Learners

New Castle County has over 19,000 multilingual learners. Delaware spends roughly $1,100 per multilingual learner — compared to $6–9K in neighboring states. Language access in civic education is not an accommodation. It is a baseline requirement.

Why: Resource gap + civic access barrier

Students with Disabilities

Civic education programs rarely include explicit accessibility design. IEP accommodations address academic content, not civic participation. This initiative requires accessibility standards to be built in at the design stage, not retrofitted.

Why: Structural exclusion from civic practice

Rural Communities

Rural Delaware students face transportation barriers to civic events, fewer local government touchpoints, and limited access to organizing infrastructure. Geographic isolation shapes civic access in ways that urban-designed programs don't account for.

Why: Geographic and infrastructure barriers

Incarcerated and System-Involved Youth

Young people who have been involved in the juvenile justice system are among those with the greatest stake in civic outcomes — and among those least likely to receive structured civic education. This initiative specifically addresses re-entry and in-facility contexts.

Why: Highest-stakes exclusion from civic systems

Tribal and Indigenous Communities

The Nanticoke Lenni-Lenape and other Indigenous communities in Delaware have distinct civic relationships with the state that standard civics curricula don't reflect. Sovereign nation relationships, treaty history, and land governance require specific curricular attention.

Why: Distinct civic relationship with state government

Immigrant Communities

Immigrant families — including mixed-status households — face real safety concerns around civic participation. Programs that don't address these barriers by design will not reach this community. Civic safety is not a peripheral concern; it is a design requirement.

Why: Safety barriers + underrepresentation

A Note on How These Communities Were Identified

Community identification was driven by three criteria: measurable gaps in civic access or educational investment, structural barriers that existing programs have not addressed, and communities with specific conditions that require tailored design — not just inclusion in a generic program.
