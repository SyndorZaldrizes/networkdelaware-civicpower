Priority Communities

WHO THIS
IS BUILT
FOR

Six communities were identified as priority focus areas based on structural gaps in civic access, representation, and educational investment. Inclusion is a design requirement — not an afterthought.

Design Principle

A framework that produces gains for the average Delaware student while leaving priority communities behind has failed its purpose.

The Six Communities

PRIORITY
FOCUS AREAS

MULTILINGUAL FAMILIES
// Language Access as Civic Infrastructure

Structural Barriers

New Castle County has over 19,000 multilingual learners. Delaware spends approximately $1,100 per multilingual learner compared to $6,000–$9,000 in neighboring states. English-only civic materials, inaccessible family engagement processes, and limited translation of school governance documents structurally exclude multilingual families from civic participation. Language access is not accommodation — it is baseline infrastructure.

Framework Response

All civic learning materials provided in English and Spanish at minimum, with a language access plan for additional languages. Professional interpretation at all community-facing events. Family voice mechanisms available in home languages. Grant eligibility tied to multilingual integration requirements.

🛡
IMMIGRANT & MIXED-STATUS FAMILIES
// Safety-Conscious Civic Engagement

Structural Barriers

Immigrant and mixed-status families face real safety concerns around civic participation. Being visible in a public civic context — presenting to a city council, leading a community meeting, being publicly quoted — carries legitimate risk that has nothing to do with civic confidence or knowledge. A civic education framework that does not address these barriers by design will not reach this community. Their exclusion is a design failure, not a personal one.

Framework Response

No-disclosure-required participation pathways. Anonymous proposal submission and voting options for all activities. Know-your-rights education integrated into facilitator training. Partnerships with trusted community organizations — La Esperanza, LACC, Delaware Hispanic Commission — as intermediaries rather than direct outreach. Multiple participation modes so public presentation is never the only path.

✊
BLACK AND BROWN YOUTH
// Organizing, Accountability, Collective Power

Structural Barriers

Black and Brown youth face school discipline disparities, over-policing, economic precarity, and fewer high-quality civic learning opportunities in under-resourced schools. Research on civic assets among youth of color identifies civic efficacy — the belief that one's actions can produce real change — as the asset most strongly associated with social activism. Critical consciousness without the accompanying efficacy produces disengagement. The mechanism for building efficacy is the experience of exercising real influence, not simulated influence.

Framework Response

Action civics with culturally sustaining curricula and student-controlled issue selection. Paid youth organizing fellowships that address economic barriers to participation. Leadership roles in real, consequential decision-making bodies. Kingian Nonviolence curriculum as civic case study centered in Black-led organizing tradition. Equity disaggregation requirements in LEA reporting to surface implementation gaps.

♿
DISABLED STUDENTS AND FAMILIES
// Accessibility as Infrastructure

Structural Barriers

Civic participation models are built around physical presence and verbal performance. Disabled students and families face ableism in civic institutions, absence of disability justice framing in curriculum, and physical, communication, and sensory accessibility gaps in civic learning settings. IEP accommodations address academic content — not civic participation. Students with disabilities are suspended and expelled at disproportionately higher rates, a structural barrier to civic participation this framework directly addresses through restorative practices.

Framework Response

Universal Design for Learning applied as a design standard, not an accommodation add-on. Disability justice as a civic history case study with contemporary relevance. Multiple communication modalities for all participation opportunities. Disabled students as co-designers of civic activities — not recipients. No comparison state fully addresses this front. Delaware has the opportunity to establish the national model.

🏳️‍🌈
LGBTQIA+ YOUTH
// Safety Before Participation

Structural Barriers

LGBTQIA+ students face safety and belonging deficits that suppress civic engagement before it begins. Hostile state and local policy environments in certain Delaware districts, absence of affirming civic spaces in most school settings, and fear of retaliation for visible participation create conditions where a civic education framework that doesn't provide a third path — beyond public exposure or withdrawal — is not serving these students. Trevor Project research (2025) documents that civic engagement correlates with better mental health outcomes among LGBTQIA+ young people when participation is safe.

Framework Response

Affirming school climate as a prerequisite for civic participation. GSA and affinity-based leadership pathways embedded in civic education design. LGBTQIA+ history and organizing as civic curriculum content. No-disclosure-required participation pathways. Explicit institutional protections against retaliation. Partnerships with Equality Delaware, United Way of Delaware PRIDE Council, and I Am Me Inc.

🌎
HISPANIC AND LATINO COMMUNITIES
// Asset-Based, Not Pan-Ethnic

Structural Barriers

Delaware's Hispanic and Latino population is not monolithic — it includes Afro-Latino, Indigenous, multi-generational, recent immigrant, and U.S.-born communities across New Castle County and agricultural Sussex County. Treating them as a single pan-ethnic bloc is itself a design failure. Research from the Latino Policy Institute documents that these communities demonstrate strong familial relationships, high educational expectations, and intergenerational civic networks — civic assets, not deficits — that are rarely recognized or leveraged by existing programs.

Framework Response

Heritage language integration in civics as an asset, not a bridge to English. Family civic literacy activities in home languages. School-community partnerships with Latino-serving organizations rather than state-led outreach. Explicit design attention to Afro-Latino and Indigenous community needs within the broader Latino category. Rejection of pan-ethnic assumptions in program design and evaluation.

The Evidence

WHY THESE
COMMUNITIES

Criterion 1

Measurable Gaps in Civic Access

Each community was identified based on documented, measurable gaps in civic access or educational investment — not assumptions. The multilingual learner funding gap, school discipline disparities, and language access barriers are empirically documented. The research base is cited in every deliverable.

Criterion 2

Structural Barriers Existing Programs Haven't Addressed

These communities face barriers that generic civic education programs consistently fail to design for. Safety-conscious participation for immigrant youth. No-disclosure pathways for LGBTQIA+ students. Multi-modal design for disabled participants. If the design doesn't start with these requirements, they don't make it in.

Criterion 3

Conditions That Require Tailored Design

Not just inclusion in a generic program — each community has specific conditions that require deliberate design choices. The framework is built around these conditions, not adapted to accommodate them after the fact. The distinction matters: retrofitted inclusion produces symbolic engagement, not real power.

Design Principle

These Are Not Deficits

Community identification was driven by systemic failures, not community deficits. Immigrant families have strong civic assets — multilingual communication, intergenerational networks, organizing experience. LGBTQIA+ youth have demonstrated civic motivation. Black and Brown youth have organizing traditions. The framework builds on these assets, not around them.
