Framework

Four strategic fronts, eight guiding principles, and the Civic Action Cycle. This is how From Civic Learning to Civic Power is structured — and how it moves.

The Strategy

Four Strategic Fronts

The framework advances four fronts at the same time. Every deliverable maps to one or more of these fronts.

Front 1

Statewide Policy + LEA Implementation

Strengthen Delaware's K–12 civics expectations at the state level while supporting Local Education Agencies in alignment, educator development, and implementation. This means pushing for a statutory mandate, dedicated funding, and accountability measures that apply to all LEAs — not just willing ones.

Front 2

Community-Rooted Power Building

Establish parent and student leadership labs, youth organizing pathways, and structured feedback mechanisms that give communities real — not symbolic — influence. Representation without power is not the goal.

Front 3

School-Based Partnerships + Hands-On Practice

Pilot hands-on civic learning through participatory budgeting, community mapping, policy simulations, and direct engagement with real decision-makers. Schools can't do this alone — community partnerships are how this scales.

Front 4

Strategic Nonviolence + Leadership Development

The differentiating argument. Equipping students to understand power, navigate conflict constructively, and build collective influence. No comparison state has written this into statute. Delaware can go first.

Design Standards

Eight Guiding Principles

  • Equity by DesignEquity requirements must be in the same legislative vehicle as the mandate — not added later.
  • Community as Co-DesignerAffected communities shape the initiative, not just receive it.
  • Action Over AbstractionCivic learning is measured by what students do, not what they can recite.
  • Decision-Maker ContactProximity to real decisions is non-negotiable for civic efficacy gains.
  • Safety and Structured InvitationParticipation gaps are supply-side failures. Remove the barriers.
  • Political HomesYoung people need organizations and spaces where they belong and can develop their voice.
  • Civic MultiplicationEvery trained participant becomes a potential trainer. Scale through people, not programs.
  • Honest ReflectionEvery action cycle ends with structural analysis, not celebration. What changed? What didn't?

The Cycle

The Civic Action Cycle

Civic power is built through a repeating loop. The cycle doesn't end at action — it ends at reflection, which feeds back into the next loop.

1. Learn → Connect civic knowledge to real community issues.
2. Analyze → Map stakeholders, identify root causes, name whose voices are missing.
3. Organize → Build the coalition. Distribute roles. Agree on a shared strategy.
4. Act → Engage a real decision-making space. Create institutional accountability.
5. Teach Back → Facilitate an experience that builds the next person's capacity.
6. Reflect & Iterate → Analyze what actually changed. Start the next cycle smarter.
