The Strategy

FOUR STRATEGIC
FRONTS

01 // Statewide Policy + LEA Implementation

STATEWIDE POLICY + LEA IMPLEMENTATION

Strengthen Delaware's K–12 civics expectations at the state level while supporting Local Education Agencies in curriculum alignment, educator development, and implementation. The goal is statewide coherence with local flexibility — but not so much flexibility that equity becomes optional.

Delaware's 2018 standards carry no statutory mandate, no dedicated funding, and no accountability structure tied to LEA implementation. The result is a system where access to quality civic education depends on which district a student attends. That is a design failure, not a funding gap.

Fellowship Deliverables — Front 1

  • Delaware Civic Education Landscape Review
  • Comparative State Analysis (MA, IL, RI, CA, NY, PR)
  • K–12 Civic Education Policy Brief
02 // Community-Rooted Power Building

COMMUNITY-ROOTED POWER BUILDING

Establish parent and student leadership labs, youth organizing pathways, and structured feedback mechanisms that give communities real — not symbolic — influence over how civic education is designed, resourced, and held accountable in their schools and districts.

CIRCLE's 2025 research shows that young people's civic trust clusters around personal networks and local institutions rather than state-level political institutions. Community infrastructure, not classroom instruction alone, is the primary intervention point.

Fellowship Deliverables — Front 2

  • Youth Civic Engagement Best Practices Memo

03 // School-Based Partnerships + Hands-On Practice

SCHOOL-BASED PARTNERSHIPS + HANDS-ON PRACTICE

Pilot hands-on civic learning models through assemblies, workshops, and leadership modules tied to real decisions — participatory budgeting, community mapping, policy simulations, and direct engagement with decision-makers. Schools cannot do this alone. Community partnerships are how this scales.

The critical distinction: action civics requires students to work with communities to influence the decisions and systems that affect them. Service learning may involve students doing things for communities. Only the former produces civic efficacy. Delaware's standards mandate neither.

Fellowship Deliverables — Front 3

  • School and Community Partnership Toolkit
  • Workshop Implementation Framework
04 // Strategic Nonviolence + Leadership Development

STRATEGIC NONVIOLENCE + LEADERSHIP DEVELOPMENT

The differentiating argument of the proposal. Most civics models emphasize knowledge or participation. This framework adds strategic nonviolence and leadership development — equipping students to understand power, navigate conflict constructively, and build collective influence through disciplined strategic action.

No comparison state has codified strategic nonviolence and leadership development in statute or integrated it into a formal civic education framework. Delaware has a genuine opportunity to lead. The evidence base is strong — the Kingian Nonviolence tradition, restorative practices research, and CIRCLE data on civic efficacy among youth of color all point in the same direction.

Design Standards

EIGHT GUIDING
PRINCIPLES

Every design decision in the framework is tested against these eight principles. Strong research findings align with most or all of them.

01
Civics as Practice, Not Just Knowledge
Does the model go beyond teaching about government — does it support students in actually engaging civic life? Knowledge is necessary but insufficient.
02
Popular Education and Lived Experience
Does it start with what students and communities already know? Does it treat them as holders of knowledge, not just recipients of instruction?
03
Truth-Telling Without Blame
Does it address historical and structural realities honestly — without assigning individual blame or moral shame? Structural analysis, not personal indictment.
04
Perspective Literacy and Critical Thinking
Does it build the ability to hold multiple perspectives while grounding analysis in evidence and context? Critical thinking that produces conclusions, not just questions.
05
Civic Participation as Power, Not Performance
Does it create real influence over decisions — or does it stop at symbolic engagement? Attending meetings, writing letters that go nowhere — these are not civic power.
06
Nonviolence as Strategy, Discipline, and Care
Does it include conflict navigation, de-escalation, or constructive collective action? Nonviolence is not passivity — it is the strategic discipline of principled civic engagement.
07
Accessibility, Dignity, and Safety by Design
Is the model built for students who face real risk from civic participation? Does it offer multiple pathways — not just public-facing ones? Safety is infrastructure.
08
Leadership as Shared and Transferable
Does it develop civic multipliers — people who share what they learn? Or does it focus only on individual leaders? Scale requires multiplication, not concentration.

The Method

THE CIVIC
ACTION CYCLE

A repeating six-stage loop. The cycle doesn't end at action — it ends at reflection, which feeds back into the next loop. Hover each stage. Explore every stage in depth →

01
LEARN
Ground civic knowledge in real community issues. Read actual policy. Map who holds power.
02
ANALYZE
Map stakeholders. Identify root causes. Ask whose voices are missing and why.
03
ORGANIZE
Build the coalition. Distribute roles. Agree on shared strategy, not just shared goals.
04
ACT
Engage a real decision-making space. Create institutional accountability — not symbolic access.
05
TEACH BACK
Facilitate an experience that builds the next person's capacity. Become a civic multiplier.
06
REFLECT + ITERATE
Analyze what actually changed. Start the next cycle smarter. Reflection is structural analysis.
