The Big Idea

What Is Civic Power?

💡

In simple words...

Civic power means being able to shape the rules and decisions that affect your life — in your school, your neighborhood, and your town. Everyone has it. Not everyone knows how to use it yet.

Civics class teaches you about government. Civic power is what you do with what you learn. Think of it like this: learning to read music is great. But civic power is actually playing the song — together, with others, in front of people who can hear you.

The 6 Steps (The Civic Action Cycle)

Civic power is built through a repeating loop of six steps. Click any step to learn more:

Step 1: Learn 🔍

Find out about a real issue in your community. Don't just read a textbook — talk to people, look at real data, and connect big ideas to things you can see in your own neighborhood.

🏫 Example: Your school doesn't have enough supplies. You learn who decides the school budget and how.

Step 2: Analyze ⚖️

Look carefully at the issue. Who has the power to change things? Who is being hurt? What are the real reasons the problem exists?

🗺 Example: You make a "power map" — a diagram showing who controls the school budget and who you'd need to convince to change it.

Step 3: Organize 🤝

You can't change things alone. Build a team, divide up jobs, agree on a shared plan. Real organizing means everyone has a role — not just one hero at the front.

👥 Example: You split into groups — one researches, one writes the proposal, one practices the presentation, one reaches out to parents.

Step 4: Act ⚡

Do the real thing. Talk to a real decision-maker. Present your research at a school board meeting. Submit a petition. Civic action means connecting to a real process, not just practicing it.

🏛 Example: You present your budget proposal to the school board and ask for a specific change. They respond — and you have a record of what they said.

Step 5: Teach Back 📣

Share what you learned with friends, family, and your community. When you teach someone else, you become a civic multiplier — someone who spreads the knowledge and the power.

🎤 Example: You run a 30-minute workshop for younger students showing them exactly what you did and how they can do it too.

Step 6: Reflect & Iterate 🔄

Honestly ask: what worked? What didn't? What would you change next time? Then start the cycle again — better and smarter than before.

✏️ Example: The board only agreed to one of your three asks. You debrief: what made that one land? Who do you need to build relationships with before the next meeting?

Words to Know

Civic Multiplier
Someone who learns civic skills and then teaches them to others, spreading power through a community.

🧒 Like a math tutor — except you're teaching people how to change the rules!

Power Map
A diagram showing who has the authority to make a decision, who is affected, and what relationships connect them.

🧒 Think of it like a map of who's in charge of what.

Action Civics
A way of learning civics by actually doing it — researching real issues, building real strategies, and talking to real decision-makers.

🧒 The difference between reading about swimming and actually jumping in.

Deliberation
Carefully discussing different sides of an issue with others before making a decision together.

🧒 When your group has a real conversation before deciding what to do — not just voting who's loudest.

Strategic Nonviolence
Using organized, peaceful action to change unjust systems — based on planning and discipline, not passivity.

🧒 It's not just being quiet. It's being smart about how you push for change.

Political Home
An organization or space where young people feel they belong, can develop their voice, and take action together.

🧒 A place where people show up for each other and help each other grow.

🎯 Mini Missions for Future Civic Leaders

Try these with a grown-up, teacher, or older sibling. None require taking a political side — they're about learning the system.

Mission 1

Find Your Representatives

With an adult, look up who represents your area in the Delaware General Assembly. What committees are they on? Have they ever voted on education?

Mission 2

Spot a Local Rule

Pick one rule at your school. Ask: why does it exist, who decided it, and how would a student change it if they wanted to?

Mission 3

Write a One-Sentence Bill

In one sentence, describe a change that could make your school, neighborhood, or city safer, fairer, or kinder.

Mission 4

Draw a Power Map

Pick an issue at your school. Draw a diagram: who decides? Who is affected? Who could be allies in pushing for change?

Mission 5

Teach Someone Something

Pick one thing you learned from this page and explain it to someone else in your own words. That makes you a civic multiplier.

Mission 6

Civic Math Corner

A class has 28 students. A bill needs more than half to pass. How many yes votes does it need? (Hint: think about what "majority" means.)

For High School Students

Civic Power Starts Now

You are old enough to participate in real civic processes — and yet most civic education programs treat you as a future citizen rather than a current one. The research is clear: the gap between wanting to participate and actually participating is not motivation. It is access, safety, and invitation.

📊

The Numbers That Matter

Among youth who voted in 2024, 22% reported protesting or attending demonstrations, compared to 8% of non-voters. The difference isn't character — it's civic ecosystem. Do you have a political home?

💬

Action Civics vs. Service Learning

Service learning does things for communities. Action civics works with communities to influence the decisions and systems that affect them. The second one builds civic efficacy. The first can reinforce paternalism.

The Six-Phase Civic Action Cycle

Learn

Connect civic knowledge to real issues in your community. Read actual policy documents. Research who holds the power over the problem you care about — and how that power was acquired.

Analyze

Map the stakeholders. Identify root causes vs. surface symptoms. Ask: whose voices are missing from the current process? What would have to change for those voices to matter?

Organize

Build the coalition. Distribute roles based on skills, not seniority. Agree on a shared strategy, not just a shared goal. Collective strategy is different from individual plans added together.

Act

Engage a real decision-making space. Testimony. Participatory budgeting. A formal proposal to a school board. The measure of the action is whether it creates institutional accountability, not whether it gets covered in a newsletter.

Teach Back

Share what you learned. Not as a summary — as a facilitated experience that builds the next person's capacity to do what you just did. This is how you become a civic multiplier.

Reflect & Iterate

Be honest. What did the action actually accomplish? What would you change? Reflection is not celebration — it's the structural analysis that makes the next cycle more effective.

For Partners, Educators, and Community Leaders

The Evidence and the Opportunity

Delaware's K–12 civics standards were last revised in 2018. They contain no statutory mandate, no dedicated funding, and no accountability mechanism tied to LEA implementation. The five comparison states with the strongest civic education systems all have something Delaware lacks: a legislative floor paired with funded infrastructure, equity disaggregation requirements, and community partnership mandates in the same vehicle.

Finding 1

Mandates Without Infrastructure Reproduce Inequity

Illinois HB 4025 created a floor, not an equitable starting point. Implementation was significantly worse in under-resourced schools — even with the mandate in place. The lesson: equity requirements must be in the same legislative vehicle as the mandate.

Finding 2

Decision-Maker Contact Is the Mechanism

Action civics research shows that civic self-efficacy gains were strongest for Black and Latino youth — and specifically driven by direct decision-maker contact. Proximity to real decisions is non-negotiable.

Finding 3

The Gap Is Supply-Side

For every form of civic action CIRCLE surveyed, more youth said they would participate if given a structured opportunity than had actually done so. The barrier is access, safety, and invitation — not motivation.
