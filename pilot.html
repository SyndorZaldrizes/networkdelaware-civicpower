The Program

NOT A
LESSON.
A SYSTEM.

"The barrier to youth civic participation is not motivation. It is access, safety, and structured invitation."

CIRCLE, 2025

This program synthesizes three high-impact models that address complementary gaps no single model covers on its own. The Student Voices / Anoka Action Civics model provides the institutional access structure: the mechanism for students to engage real civic authorities with real proposals. Justice University, developed by the Gathering for Justice, provides the organizing architecture: collective strategy, campaign design, and the train-the-trainer pipeline that creates civic multipliers rather than just participants. Kingian Nonviolence training provides the conflict infrastructure: tools for navigating institutional resistance, managing power dynamics, and sustaining disciplined collective action over time.

Each session in the six-session arc maps to a stage of the Civic Action Cycle: Learn, Analyze, Organize, Act, Teach Back, and Reflect. The cycle does not end at action. It ends at reflection, which feeds the next loop. That structure is what separates programs that build durable civic capacity from programs that produce a single presentation and stop.

The leadership pipeline is the feature that makes this self-sustaining. Every participant who completes the arc is a potential co-facilitator in the next cohort. Every co-facilitator who completes the practicum becomes a lead facilitator capable of running the program independently. Over time, the program's cost of delivery decreases as internal capacity grows. That is not just an efficiency argument. It is the mechanism by which civic power actually multiplies.

The Structure

SIX-SESSION ARC

Each session builds on the previous one. The arc runs 6 to 8 weeks. A 90-minute standalone session is also available as an entry point for organizations exploring participation before committing to the full program.

Session 01 LEARN

Grounding Civic Knowledge in Reality

Participants identify a real issue in their school or community and begin connecting it to actual policy documents and public records. Community listening assignments start between sessions. Power mapping is introduced as a tool, not a concept. The goal is to close the distance between civics-as-abstraction and civics-as-something-that-shapes-your-life.

Front 1 + Front 2
Session 02 ANALYZE

Power Mapping with a Community Partner

A real community partner joins the session to give direct feedback on participants' analysis. Students build a full stakeholder map: who holds decision-making authority, who is most affected, who are potential allies, and where leverage exists. The community partner provides firsthand context that no textbook can replicate.

Front 2 + Front 3
Session 03 ORGANIZE

Nonviolence as Organizing Strategy

The Six Steps of Kingian Nonviolence are introduced as an organizing framework, not a history lesson. Participants apply them to their specific issue. The session includes structured role-play where one group plays an institutional representative who resists the participants' ask. Practicing de-escalation and strategic response before the real action is what separates programs that prepare students from programs that expose them.

Front 4
Session 04 ORGANIZE + ACT

Strategy and Campaign Design

Participants develop a full campaign strategy: issue statement, theory of change, specific ask, target institution, stakeholder map, action timeline, and contingency plan. The concept of civic leverage is introduced: the difference between an ask a decision-maker can safely ignore and one tied to institutional accountability, public visibility, or political cost. Groups present strategies to peers for structured feedback.

Front 1 + Front 3
Session 05 ACT

Real Civic Action

Participants carry out the action they designed. Depending on available institutional relationships, this means presenting to a school board committee, delivering a community survey to a council member, organizing a community listening session, or submitting a formal policy recommendation. Facilitators are present but do not intervene. The institution responds. That response, whatever it is, becomes material for the next session.

Front 3
Session 06 TEACH BACK + REFLECT

Multiplying Civic Capacity

The session opens with a 30-minute teach-back workshop designed and facilitated by participants for a new audience: younger students, family members, or community members who were not part of the original cohort. The second half is an honest full-arc debrief: what the action accomplished, what it did not, what would change next time. Participants who want to step into the co-facilitator role in the next cohort make that commitment here.

Front 2 + Front 4

The Infrastructure

THREE-TIER
LEADERSHIP
PIPELINE

The pipeline is what separates a program from an infrastructure. Each tier reduces the program's dependence on external staffing and expands its geographic reach. A program that can only run when an outside facilitator is available is a program with a ceiling. This one is designed without one.

01 // Tier One

PARTICIPANT

Complete the six-session arc. Leave with a power map, a civic action on record, a teach-back delivered, and a structured debrief. Every participant who goes through this cycle is a civic multiplier: someone with firsthand experience organizing collective action and the vocabulary to explain it to others.

Entry point for all participants
02 // Tier Two

OBSERVER-ANALYST

Return to a subsequent cohort as a co-facilitator. Take structured notes on facilitation decisions, group dynamics, and strategic choices. Debrief with the lead facilitator after each session. Run specific segments under supervision. As the number of Tier Two participants grows, the program's reliance on external facilitation decreases.

Reduces per-cohort external cost
03 // Tier Three

LEAD FACILITATOR

Complete a 20-to-30-hour practicum aligned with the Kingian Nonviolence training-of-trainers framework. Qualify to run new cohorts independently. Each Tier Three facilitator is a statewide scalability node: someone who can start a cohort in their school or community organization without waiting for Network Delaware to send staff.

30-hour practicum + Kingian certification track

The Research

EVIDENCE
BASE

Every design decision in this program traces back to a specific empirical finding. The five findings below are the most directly relevant to the program's structure and priority communities. The full research base is available in the Resource Library.

Research using structural equation modeling with 903 urban middle and high school students found that the relationship between exposure to democratic practices and civic self-efficacy was significantly stronger for Black and Latino youth than for white students. The mechanism is direct decision-maker contact: safety-themed projects with decision-maker engagement produced the most robust gains in future civic commitment.

The I-ACTED cluster randomized controlled trial (BMC Public Health, 2025) confirms that action civics produces positive wellbeing outcomes through civic connection and empowerment. These effects are not uniformly distributed. Under-resourced schools remain systematically underserved. Session 05 of this program is designed specifically around that finding: not simulated decision-making, but actual institutional contact with a required response.

Cohen et al., PMC, 2017 / I-ACTED RCT, BMC Public Health, 2025

A mixed-methods quasi-experimental study of the Kingian Nonviolence Conflict Reconciliation Training Program with high school students found that the program was associated with improved social and cultural learning outcomes and reduced rates of violence among participants. The study introduced the concept of cultural and emotional learning as a distinct outcome domain, using a pretest/posttest design with four one-month follow-up focus groups.

For students from the priority communities this program serves, those findings are not incidental. Strategic nonviolence education is not remedial programming. It is a proactive investment in the forms of civic engagement these students already believe in, built around the documented methodology of movements they have direct historical and personal stakes in.

Cyril, University of Rhode Island, 2020

CIRCLE defines a political home as an organization or space that provides a sense of belonging, opportunities to develop voice, opportunities to learn, opportunities to take action, and relationships with adults who support civic participation. Youth with political homes are dramatically more engaged across all 17 civic engagement measures CIRCLE tracks. Among 2024 youth voters, 27 percent reported being part of a movement or organized group, compared to 13 percent of non-voters.

This is the finding that motivates the community partnership model in Session 02 and the teach-back structure in Session 06. The program is not designed to be the political home. It is designed to build the conditions for political homes to form around the participants and their issues after the program ends.

CIRCLE, Growing Voters Framework, 2023 / CIRCLE Post-Election Data, 2026

CIRCLE's multi-year evaluation of Illinois HB 4025, the most comprehensive statutory civics mandate in the comparison field, found that implementation quality was significantly higher in schools with peer mentor support and significantly lower in under-resourced schools serving predominantly Black and Brown students, even with the mandate in place. The statute created a floor, not an equitable starting point.

This finding directly informs the pilot program's design priorities: geographic distribution across all three Delaware counties, deliberate site selection that includes at least one school or organization serving a high proportion of students from the six priority communities, and equity disaggregation requirements built into the evaluation framework from day one rather than added after the fact.

CIRCLE, HB 4025 Evaluation / Illinois Civics Hub Research Practice Partnership

The comparative analysis conducted for this initiative reviewed six jurisdictions: Massachusetts, Illinois, Rhode Island, California, New York, and Puerto Rico. None has codified strategic nonviolence and leadership development in statute or integrated it into a formal civic education framework. The closest institutional reference point is Nonviolent Schools RI, which implements the Kingian curriculum as a school climate intervention. It exists alongside civic education policy, not within it.

Governor Matt Meyer's March 2026 Civic Learning Week proclamation and the Delaware Civics Education Coalition's active organizing capacity represent a policy window. This pilot is designed to generate the documented implementation evidence that makes the legislative case. The program's evaluation framework is built to produce exactly that kind of evidence, not just attendance data.

Network Delaware Comparative State Analysis, 2025

Who This Serves

PRIORITY
COMMUNITIES

These six communities were identified based on documented, measurable gaps in civic access, not assumptions about capability or interest. Every design decision in the program, from the no-disclosure participation pathways to the multilingual materials requirement, addresses a specific structural barrier that generic civic education programs have consistently failed to design around. Inclusion is infrastructure here, not accommodation.

🛡

Immigrant + Mixed-Status Families

Being visible in a public civic context carries real risk for mixed-status families that has nothing to do with motivation or knowledge. Every activity in the program offers a pathway that does not require public identification. Anonymous proposal submission, voting options, and written alternatives are built into the design, not offered as exceptions.

Partners: La Esperanza, Latin American Community Center (LACC)
✊

Black and Brown Youth

Civic education that teaches democratic ideals without addressing the structural conditions participants already navigate does not serve these students. Issue selection is student-controlled. The Kingian Nonviolence curriculum grounds civic action in a tradition of Black-led organizing, not as a historical add-on but as the analytical and strategic foundation of the program. Structural inequity is named, not avoided.

Design principle: Student-controlled issue selection, Kingian grounding
🌎

Hispanic + Latino Communities

Delaware's Hispanic and Latino population is not a single community. It includes Afro-Latino, Indigenous, multi-generational, recent immigrant, and U.S.-born residents across New Castle and Sussex counties. Treating them as a unified bloc is a design failure. Materials are available in Spanish. Facilitation can be bilingual. Community organizations with existing trust relationships lead outreach, not the program.

Design principle: Internal diversity respected, Spanish materials standard
♿

Disabled Students + Families

Universal Design for Learning is applied as a design standard from the start, not retrofitted. Every session offers multiple participation modes. Disability justice is a civic history case study in the curriculum, not a sensitivity note. Disabled students are co-designers of activities within their cohort, not recipients of a program designed around able-bodied assumptions.

Design principle: UDL as infrastructure, disability justice as civic content
🏳️‍🌈

LGBTQIA+ Youth

Safety has to be a design prerequisite, not a program consideration. Group norms in Session 01 explicitly name respect for identity. No self-identification is required for any participation pathway. The community partner for Session 02 includes at least one affirming organization. Research from the Trevor Project documents that civic engagement correlates with better mental health outcomes for LGBTQIA+ youth when participation is structurally safe, not just nominally welcoming.

Partners: Equality Delaware, United Way of Delaware PRIDE Council, I Am Me Inc.
🗣

Multilingual Families

Language access is civic infrastructure. Not an accommodation offered if requested, but a baseline design requirement applied to every session from the beginning. All written materials are available in English and Spanish at minimum. Professional interpretation is provided at all family-facing events. New Castle County's approximately 19,000 multilingual learners receive $1,100 per learner from the state. That gap is the context for this requirement.

Design principle: English + Spanish minimum, interpretation standard

The Plan

THREE-PHASE
ROLLOUT

01 Pilot Year 1

DOCUMENTATION BEFORE SCALE

Pilot 3 to 5 sites across all three Delaware counties. At least one site must serve a majority of students from the six priority communities. At least one must be rural. The goal of Year 1 is not to reach as many students as possible. It is to document what works, what requires adaptation, and what the Delaware context specifically requires. That documentation becomes the curriculum refinement and the evidence base for Phase 2 expansion.

3-5 Sites One Per County Evaluation Built In Equity Disaggregation
02 Expansion Years 2-3

TRAINED FACILITATORS, EXPANDED SITES

Use pilot documentation to refine the curriculum. Train a cohort of 15 to 20 lead facilitators from both school and community settings. Expand to 10 to 15 implementation sites. Establish a statewide facilitator learning community: a regular gathering for lead facilitators to share practice, identify emerging leaders for Tier Three, and troubleshoot implementation challenges across different institutional contexts.

15-20 Lead Facilitators 10-15 Sites Facilitator Learning Community Curriculum Refinement
03 Infrastructure Years 3-5

SELF-SUSTAINING INFRASTRUCTURE

Full pipeline activation: Tier Three facilitators leading new cohorts, the learning community operating as peer support and quality assurance, LEA partnerships integrating the six-session arc into district capstone requirements. At this phase the program should be self-sustaining: new lead facilitators developing faster than existing ones cycle out, institutional partnerships maintained through documented protocols rather than individual relationships. That is what infrastructure looks like.

Pipeline Self-Sustaining LEA Capstone Integration Policy Evidence Generated Statewide Reach

THREE WAYS

// For Schools + LEAs

PILOT PARTNER SCHOOL

Commit to hosting one cohort in Year 1. Network Delaware provides the facilitator, all curriculum materials in English and Spanish, and institutional partnership coordination. The time commitment is six sessions over 6 to 8 weeks. We handle the logistics. You provide the students, a designated staff liaison, and a willingness to connect the cohort's action to a real school-level or district-level decision-making process.

Contact us to discuss your site
// For Community Organizations

COMMUNITY PARTNER

Serve as the Session 02 community partner, connecting students to the real institutional landscape around their issue. Provide trusted intermediary access to families who may not engage with school-led programs. Receive trained civic educators from the program's leadership pipeline who can run activities within your organization. Community partners are co-designers here, not guests.

Contact us to discuss your organization's role
// For Funders + Institutions

INVEST IN THE INFRASTRUCTURE

Year 1 funding covers facilitator training, curriculum development, materials translation, site coordination, and the evaluation framework that generates the evidence base for Phase 2. Year 2 and 3 funding builds the facilitator cohort and the learning community. By Year 5 this program does not need external facilitation to run. That is what you are investing in: not a program that needs perpetual support, but one that builds the capacity to sustain itself.
