// Core Argument

Delaware students are taught about democracy but are not prepared to practice it. This initiative addresses that gap by building civic capacity; the ability to think critically, organize collectively, make decisions, navigate conflict, and empower others.

The Initiative

WHAT THIS IS

From Civic Learning to Civic Power is a statewide K–12 civic education initiative developed by Network Delaware. It is built on the argument that Delaware students are not being prepared to practice democracy, only to read about it. The initiative proposes a multi-front strategy to close that gap, with specific attention to communities that existing programs have consistently failed to reach.

The work is organized around a four-front framework, a six-phase Civic Action Cycle, eight guiding equity principles, and six identified priority communities. All deliverables are grounded in primary research, CIRCLE data, and analysis of six comparison jurisdictions: Massachusetts, Illinois, Rhode Island, California, New York, and Puerto Rico.

This site serves as the public-facing home for the fellowship's research, tools, and policy documents designed to be usable by school administrators, community organizations, educators, funders, and students alike.

The Central Gap

Fewer than 1 in 4 eighth graders nationally demonstrate proficiency in civics. Delaware's standards contain no statutory mandate, no dedicated funding, and no accountability tied to LEA implementation. The gap is structural, not motivational.

What's Different

Most civic education frameworks treat nonviolent strategy and leadership development as optional add-ons. This initiative treats them as a required core component; the differentiating argument and is the first to propose codifying it in statute.

The Measure of Success

Success is measured by what participants can do, analyze, and teach others not by attendance counts or program exposure. Civic capacity, not civic exposure, is the goal.
